The point of any and every calendar is to orient us within, not beyond, the world we inherit. Although science fictions and social utopias work to the rhythms of calendars that are at once fantasy and format, in that their calendars are excuses for imaginative leaps, the calendars that we live by, day to day, tend rather to anchor us to a world we must live in from moment to moment. However, the idea of consulting a calendar every day has been, until recently, the habit exclusively of the upper ranks of clergy and corporate executives; only within the last two hundred years have ordinary folks been compelled (or felt the necessity) to consult calendars each day, and so calendars have come to seem to be burdensome, an imposition and a heaviness of time-- so that, only now, do we feel that "there is not enough time" (a curious, and strangely modern, notion) or that time lies witrhin a grid that is a straitjacket. A calendar that counts down toward the millennium emphasizes the burdensomeness of time; we should rather want to count across the turn of the millennium, and so open up our lives to worlds of possibility.
Hillel Schwartz - Millennium Institute Senior Fellow


Finally, this is not a feedback, but equally relevant: at the telecast of the Emmy Awards of The Academy of Television Arts and Science, on Sept. 13, 1998, 10 different actors introduced the 10 most important events in television history. The #1 event was Neil Armstrong walking on the moon, introduced by Tom Hanks, who later in the evening won an Emmy as the executive producer of "From the Earth to the Moon". In his speech, Tom told that the science-fiction writer Ray Bradbury suggested that we should change our calendars, making July twentieth 1969 the first day of a new age.
